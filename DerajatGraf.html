Degree

25 February 2025 by Riski

Previously, we have learned that a graph is a set of points called vertices connected by lines called edges. In this article, we will discuss the degree of a vertex in a graph. The degree of a vertex is the number of edges incident to that vertex. The degree of a vertex $v$ is denoted by $d(v)$. For example, suppose we have a graph $G = (V, E)$ with $V = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ and $E = \{\{1, 2\}, \{1, 3\}, \{1, 4\}, \{2, 3\}, \{2, 4\}, \{3, 4\}, \{3, 5\}\}$. Then, the degree of vertex 1 is 3, the degree of vertex 2 is 3, the degree of vertex 3 is 4, the degree of vertex 4 is 3, and the degree of vertex 5 is 1.

There is an interesting fact about the degree of a vertex in a graph. The sum of all vertex degrees in any undirected and simple graph is always even. This is expressed using the following theorem.

Theorem 1. (Handshaking Theorem) The sum of all vertex degrees in any undirected and simple graph is always even.

Proof: Let $G = (V, E)$ be an undirected and simple graph. Then, each edge connects two vertices. Therefore, each edge will add 1 to the degree of the vertices connected by that edge. Thus, the sum of all vertex degrees in graph $G$ is twice the sum of all edges in graph $G$. Since $G$ is an undirected and simple graph, each edge is counted only once. Therefore, the sum of all vertex degrees in graph $G$ is even. $\blacksquare$
